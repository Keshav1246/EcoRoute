01 — The Challenge

Delhi's Air Quality Crisis

Delhi ranks among the world's most polluted cities, with PM2.5 levels frequently exceeding 300 μg/m³ — 5× the safe limit.

🏭

The Problem

Municipal planners lack tools to identify micro-level hotspots where interventions would have maximum impact. Existing solutions provide only city-level aggregates.

📊

Ingest & Transform

Ingest and transform multi-source environmental telemetry data from traffic APIs and air quality sensors.

🎯

Identify Hotspots

Identify top emission hotspots across 7 key Delhi transit corridors with composite Emission Risk Score.

🔗

Correlate Data

Correlate traffic congestion with air quality degradation using weighted scoring methodology.

📈

Executive Dashboards

Provide executive-level dashboards for municipal planners with real-time cross-filtering capabilities.

Emission Risk Score (ERS) Formula

ERS = (0.5 × PM2.5norm) + (0.3 × Congestionnorm) + (0.2 × AQInorm)
PM2.5 — 50% Primary health indicator
Congestion — 30% Vehicle emissions correlator
AQI — 20% Composite air quality metric

# Lambda handler — fetch from TomTom & OpenAQ APIs
def lambda_handler(event, context):
    timestamp = datetime.now(ZoneInfo("Asia/Kolkata"))
    partition = f"year={timestamp.year}/month={timestamp.month:02d}/day={timestamp.day:02d}"
    
    for location in DELHI_STATIONS:
        traffic_data = fetch_tomtom_traffic(location['lat'], location['lon'])
        aqi_data = fetch_openaq_data(location['lat'], location['lon'])
        
        s3.put_object(
            Bucket='ecoroute-bronze',
            Key=f"traffic/{partition}/{location['name']}_{timestamp.isoformat()}.json",
            Body=json.dumps(traffic_data)
        )

# Haversine formula — spatial join within 15km radius
joined = joined.withColumn('dist_km', (
    F.acos(
        F.sin(F.radians(F.col('latitude'))) * F.sin(F.radians(F.col('aqi_lat'))) +
        F.cos(F.radians(F.col('latitude'))) * F.cos(F.radians(F.col('aqi_lat'))) *
        F.cos(F.radians(F.col('aqi_lon') - F.col('longitude')))
    ) * F.lit(6371)
))

# Emission Risk Score computation
final = final.withColumn('ERS', F.round(
    (F.col('congestion_score') * F.col('rush_weight')) + F.col('delta_aqi') / 50, 2
))

SQL — Top Emission Hotspots Query 
-- Query the Gold layer for emission rankings
SELECT location, 
       ROUND(AVG(congestion_score), 2) AS avg_congestion,
       ROUND(AVG(pm25), 2) AS avg_pm25,
       aqi_category,
       ROUND(AVG(ERS), 2) AS avg_emission_risk_score
FROM   "ecoroute_db"."full_correlation"
GROUP BY location, aqi_category
ORDER BY avg_emission_risk_score DESC;

05 — Insights

Key Findings

Actionable insights derived from the analysis of over 1 million data points.

01

ITO: The Critical Hotspot

ITO has the highest ERS of 2.51 despite moderate PM2.5 (159.59 μg/m³). Congestion is the dominant factor with avg score of 2.07.

Recommendation Traffic signal optimization and congestion pricing at this central junction would have outsized impact.
02

The Rohini Paradox

Rohini has the highest PM2.5 (300 μg/m³) — double the hazardous threshold — yet ranks 3rd in ERS due to low congestion (1.59).

Recommendation Focus on industrial emission controls rather than traffic management.
03

Lajpat Nagar — Best Performer

Lowest ERS of 1.0, demonstrating that suburban areas have significantly lower combined emission risk.

Recommendation Use as a best-practice benchmark for achieving low emission zones.
04

151% ERS Variance

ERS ranges from 1.0 to 2.51 indicating significant spatial heterogeneity in emission patterns across Delhi.

Implication Blanket citywide restrictions are inefficient. Zone-specific interventions are needed.
06 — Technical Deep Dive

Technical Challenges & Solutions

📏

Haversine Radius Calibration

Problem: Choosing the right spatial join radius for the Haversine formula. A 5km radius matched too few AQI stations — most locations had sparse or no sensor data. A 25km radius pulled in distant stations with no real correlation, diluting the data quality.

Solution: After iterative testing, 10km radius was the optimal balance — capturing enough nearby AQI stations for statistical significance while excluding unrelated far-away sensors.

5 km Too Small Sparse data, missing stations
10 km Optimal ✓ Strong correlation, sufficient coverage
25 km Too Large Diluted data, unrelated stations 
# Filter spatial join to 10km radius
joined_filtered = joined.filter(F.col('dist_km') <= 10.0)
# Result: 7 stations with 2-4 AQI sensors each
🔀

TOPN Tie-Breaker

Problem: "Best AQI Location" KPI showed blank when multiple locations had identical PM2.5 (159.59).

Root Cause: TOPN returned multiple rows; SELECTEDVALUE() returned BLANK().

Best AQI Location = 
CALCULATE(
    MIN(Leaderboard[location]),  -- alphabetical tie-breaking
    TOPN(1, ALL(Leaderboard), Leaderboard[avg_pm25], ASC)
)
📐

Dual-Axis Scale

Problem: PM2.5 (100-300) and ERS (0-10) on same chart squashed ERS line flat.

Solution: Used Dual-Axis Combo Chart with independent Y-axes to preserve visual clarity for both metrics.
